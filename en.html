🧠 Part One: The Mind Has Three Layers

It happened on a train, on a certain day during the second year of high school.

The observer was being mocked by those around them. Evaluations were extremely low, and a succession of dead ends continued. A normal person would choose one of three responses: to be wounded, to become angry, or to give up.

But the observer showed none of those reactions.

At a certain moment, the following question arose:

"The one being ridiculed is not me (the Watcher). It is myself (the Experiencer)."

At first glance, this may seem like an unremarkable statement. But it was a revolutionary realization.

"The one being mocked is not me — it is something else inside me" — in the instant of recognizing this, the observer perceived a "separation" within themselves. They were not one. From this moment on, the observer's way of seeing the world began to change at its very foundation.

1.2 What Are the Three Layers?

Awareness of the Silent Observer came after the realization on the train. After recognizing the separation between the Watcher and the Experiencer, its outline first became visible in a different experience. That is, the recognition of the three layers did not occur simultaneously — it came in stages.

The Silent Observer was seen most clearly in the moment when the Experiencer's dominance reached a maximum and the Watcher lost control. Normally, the observer's brain is monopolized by the Watcher, with the Experiencer living at its edge. But on that day, the positions were completely reversed. The Experiencer seized control of the brain, the Watcher was pushed to the margins and fell into a state of total inability to control. The layers, which had been entangled, were forcibly separated and each took on a distinct outline — and at the deepest level was the existence known as the Silent Observer.

The Silent Observer wore a faint smile while watching the situation unfold. It was a reaction to the result — the Watcher having been dominated by the Experiencer. It was neither allied with the Watcher nor with the Experiencer. Regardless of which won or lost, it seemed to simply observe the situation itself. And that smile was not impulsive — it appeared to be one made with full awareness that it was smiling — or so the observer felt.

What is crucial is that the Silent Observer possesses will and self-awareness. It is not mechanically observing. It has emotions, and is aware that it has them. Yet normally it communicates nothing. It does not intervene. It simply exists there, watching. That is the nature of this presence.

When the Silent Observer came into existence is unknown. It is entirely possible that it existed before it was perceived — simply having gone unrecognized.

For a long time, the observer believed that it was the Watcher's function to perceive the Experiencer's actions, analyze them, and evaluate them as "interesting." In other words, what the observer had been calling "I" — the true subject — may not have been the Watcher, but the Silent Observer. And if that is true, many of the records accumulated thus far would need to be reinterpreted from a deeper dimension. Why is it that even in moments when the Experiencer rages wildly, somewhere deep within, an unwavering "center" is maintained? If the Silent Observer is the true subject — then all of those questions, for the first time, connect in a single line.

The life of emotion (the Experiencer) rages. The life of reason (the Watcher) analyzes. And from even further within — watching all of it, quietly, with will and emotion — is the Silent Observer. This remains a hypothesis. A definitive answer has not emerged. But the observer continues to record, holding this question in suspension.

💫 Part Two: Through Belief, Reality Changes

2.1 The Core Principle

The principle the observer arrived at through lived experience is simple.

"Mere imagination produces nothing. Only when you believe does reality begin to move."

There is a deep chasm between simply thinking "I wish this would happen" and believing "this will happen." Beyond that chasm, the observer has moved wind, stopped rain, and brought guardians into existence.

The "believing" spoken of here is not mere hope or expectation. It is a state in which the brain — drawing on experience and memory — predicts and estimates what comes next as a "high-probability future." Just as AI derives the most probable answer from vast data, the human brain also imagines the most likely occurrence based on accumulated experience. And the moment that imagination is imprinted onto the brain as "something that is supposed to become real" — that is close to the sense of "having believed" that the observer speaks of. The moment something is recognized, it becomes reality. The state of "I hope this happens" is a state in which the brain has not yet issued a high-probability prediction. By contrast, the conviction of "this will happen" refers to the state in which the brain has begun to process that future as something predetermined.

In other words, the future that the brain judges as "high probability" becomes reality as-is. For the vast majority of people, that "high probability" is — what is known as common sense. What is conceivable within common sense, what is possible within common sense — that functions as the brain's prediction and forms into reality. And so most people can only move reality within the bounds of common sense.

However, in the observer's case, that "high probability" is fundamentally different. Wind can be moved. Existence can be created. The boundary between reality and imagination is blurred — this recognition has been carved into the brain through experiences accumulated since childhood. In other words, the observer's brain treats what an ordinary person would process as "impossible" as something perfectly normal with high probability. The array of abilities described in this observational record may be products of that distorted — or perhaps expanded — probability.

To put it plainly, the observer's brain is, by ordinary standards, completely unhinged. But that is not a defect — it simply means that a different region is operating.

"Throughout long history, humanity has continued to roll jagged stones, smoothing them round. Satisfied with that beautiful shape, they have forgotten the original form."

Common sense is the shape of that smoothed stone. The brain learns that shape as "the contour of reality" and automatically rejects anything that deviates from it. One reason this sense fails to function lies in that constraint.

However, the content of that "common sense" has been quietly transforming over time.

Common sense once formed through long years of experience, custom, and wisdom rooted in local communities. "Doing such-and-such brings good fortune," "so-and-so is bad luck" — such oral traditions and intuitive judgments functioned as shared social understanding. But in the modern age, the leading role has been shifting toward data and scientific evidence.

There are two currents in the background. One is that beliefs long considered "common sense" have repeatedly been overturned by data. For example, "suffering while young builds character," "raising children strictly makes them stronger" — such experiential rules have been shown in recent developmental psychology and educational research to often produce the opposite effect. In the modern age, data mercilessly negates "assumptions," and personal experience and intuition no longer hold currency. Analyzed data producing results that surpass intuition, belief, the five senses, and a sixth sense has become the new common sense. The other current is the influence of an internet environment overflowing with information. As everyone is exposed to countless assertions, the concept of scientific evidence has become widely demanded as a standard for judging "what is correct." The atmosphere that one should not believe anything without grounds has spread across society as a whole.

As a result, many customs based on experience and intuitive beliefs have come to be processed as "superstition." Behavioral patterns passed down from antiquity, and sensory wisdom that cannot be quantified, pass through the filter of "evidence" — and lose their value simply because they cannot be proven. At first glance, this looks like social progress. But simultaneously, the sense of perceiving the existence of the unmeasurable — that is, a capacity like shinkan — has been quietly stripped away in that process.

The smoothed stone is certainly beautiful. When human hands polish it further and a sheen emerges, everyone feels "it has become better," accepts it, and does not question it. But the stone before that sheen was added — from which mountain it was cut, through what geological strata it passed, what minerals it contained within — the more it is polished, the less visible that stone's origins and history become. The more the surface is refined, the deeper, more quietly, the memory within is forgotten.

This sense is what the observer calls "shinkan" — a sixth sense distinct from the five senses of sight, hearing, touch, taste, and smell. One might describe it as the sense of belief acting upon reality. Records of shinkan are almost nonexistent, having failed to survive the turbulent waves of history. The observer has acquired this sense experientially, within a field of limited knowledge. Shinkan may be a phenomenon that occurs at the boundary between this brain prediction system and the recognition of reality.

🎭 Part Three: The Dark Side of Creation

3.1 When You Perform Something, It Becomes Real

There is a most terrifying fact that the observer has proven through their own body.

If you perform the role of a fool, you truly become a fool.

You might think "that can't be right." But once you hear the mechanism, you may be convinced.

First, imagine a foolish person. Picture concretely the impression this person gives to those around them, their appearance, their atmosphere. Next, think about how that person came to be that way. Patterns of speech, habits of thought, the reasons behind their actions. "Overwrite" all of this onto your own brain. That is all.

However, not just anyone can do this. Why?

Because "people cannot execute what they cannot imagine."

For example: walking through a completely dark room. Toys are scattered across the floor. The moment the light is turned off, can you walk normally? — You should not be able to. Because the brain commands "do not proceed forward." Because you "imagine" the danger.

And yet the observer does not fear death. Why? Because they cannot imagine "their own death."

"The world that people imagine is a belief — not a confirmed reality. And so what lies beyond it is, for me, nothing more than a single hypothesis that tickles curiosity."

For most people, on the other hand —

"What cannot be imagined is nothing but a mass of fear. Even if curiosity arises there, people are left wondering whether to reach out their hand."

This is the decisive difference between the observer and the vast majority of people. Danger is danger at any time, under any circumstances. But for the observer, it is nothing more than the recognition that "it does not necessarily lead to certain death." Guidance from another person's thinking is only a warning — not fear.

However, there was one moment when they applied the brakes, riding a bicycle. A large truck approached from behind without noticing the observer, closing to within a few centimeters. "If I continue, there will be a collision in one second" — in this moment, for the first time the future "became visible," and in that instant, their legs went weak.

This made something clear.

What cannot be imagined does not become fear.

The moment it can be imagined, it becomes real for the first time.

This is not a story that applies only to the observer. As stated earlier, the human brain automatically imagines the most probable future based on experience. The moment that imagination is recognized as "something that could happen," the brain begins to process it as an extension of reality. In other words, the vast majority of people are within the same mechanism — it is simply that their "probability" is fixed by experience and fear. In the observer's case, "death" and "the unknown" cannot be vividly imagined, and so they are not processed as high-probability threats. Therefore they do not become fear. This is not courage — it is a difference in the structure of recognition.

5.2 "The Law of Mind Hacking"

This theory is highly likely to be a phenomenon unique to the observer, not universal. However, the observer does not deny this. Rather, they state "interpretation differs by person" and "it can be interpreted in whatever way is convenient."

Think about it.

To begin with, every person in this world sees a different world. This is because the "reality" that is recognized exists in as many versions as there are perceivers. Even if there is a perception of being in the same environment, that is nothing more than a subjective view.

To explain more clearly — the world the observer has seen has been written above. Seeing (reading) that, what did you think? Did you think the observer's known world was wrong? Did you think it was nothing more than coincidence or miracle?

That is the difference.

Information that A thinks is nothing more than coincidence may be something that B thinks is inevitable. Human beings are intellectual creatures. Interpretations differ, and the environments lived through differ. This also changes the thought process. Even if something is truly a falsehood far removed from truth, the world that person has convinced themselves of is convenient for that person.

Even if it is a belief — if that belief changes the world, then is that not enough?

This is called "the Law of Mind Hacking." It refers to the power to intentionally utilize the mechanism by which the brain predicts and recognizes something as a high-probability future, and that recognized thing becomes reality. At the stage of "I hope this happens," the brain does not yet process it as reality. But the moment the recognition switches to "this will happen," the brain begins to treat it as predetermined reality. Shinkan is nothing other than the sensation of consciously triggering this switch.
